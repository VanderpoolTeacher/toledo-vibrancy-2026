# Toledo Economic Development Brief — May 19, 2026

**Window covered:** Approximately the last 48 hours, with carry-forward context on slower-moving stories still in motion.
**Coverage areas:** Downtown · Warehouse District · Vistula / Ostrich Towne · Uptown · Junction · Citywide grants and business development

## Top story: 19 Vibrancy Initiative grants awarded across 10 neighborhoods

On May 15, Mayor Wade Kapszukiewicz and Chief Growth Officer Brandon Sehlhorst announced the 2026 Vibrancy Initiative grant awards, funding 19 redevelopment projects across 10 Toledo neighborhoods. The awards draw from a $630,000 pool but are expected to catalyze roughly $13.5 million in commercial building improvements — storefront facade work, reactivation of long-vacant ground-floor spaces, and tenant-ready buildouts. The Department received 82 applications across the program's three tracks (White Box, Facade Improvement, Planning Grant) and selected 19 based on the Vibrancy Incentive Review Committee's recommendations.

The highlights matrix: 11 awards on Legacy Commercial Corridors, 10 commercial facades to be restored, 10 long-vacant storefronts reactivated, 8 existing businesses supported, and 5 new businesses attracted.

**Why it matters:** This is the single largest concentrated burst of small-business reinvestment money the city distributes each year, and the 22× leverage ratio ($13.5M private spend on $630K public spend) is the metric to watch — it's how the city justifies the program politically. If you're tracking neighborhood business districts, the specific 19 properties are the leading indicator of where activity will visibly shift over the next 12–18 months.

## Warehouse District

### Huron Yards phase two nearing completion
Crane Development's Huron Yards project is on track to wrap phase two within 2026. The former Alleyway building at 28 N. Erie St. is being renovated into a new restaurant called **The Station**, and a grocery store is taking the ground floor of the old Okun Produce building. Resident parking and the upcoming beer garden's site work are already finished.

**Why it matters:** A grocery store in the Warehouse District is the missing-amenity piece that consistently turns downtown-adjacent districts from "lively at night" into "lively all day." It also strengthens the case for the next residential phase by making downtown living viable without a car trip. Track the grocery operator announcement — that detail will tell you whether this is targeting downtown residents or a broader catchment.

### Port of Toledo seeks new warehouse and liquid terminal
The Blade reported (April 13) that the Toledo-Lucas County Port Authority is pursuing a new warehouse and liquid bulk terminal to expand cargo handling capacity.

**Why it matters:** Port investments are slower-moving than retail/restaurant news but are the strongest indicator of industrial-side economic capacity. A liquid terminal in particular signals confidence in long-term industrial demand and may anchor downstream logistics jobs in adjacent neighborhoods.

## Vistula District / Ostrich Towne

### Ostrich Towne anchors continue to come online
The $17 million Ostrich Towne project — the warehouse-conversion district inside historic Vistula — now has two operating tenants: Molly's Irish Pub and Avestruz Tapas & Tequila. WTOL recently profiled how the small business operators there are using the "making yourself a destination" playbook to draw foot traffic into a district that doesn't yet have organic walk-by demand.

**Why it matters:** Ostrich Towne is the test case for whether a "create a district from scratch" play works in Toledo's downtown-adjacent geography. Two tenants is enough to start drawing comparison shoppers; the next 2–3 lease signings will determine whether the district hits critical mass or stays a curiosity. Watch for any Vibrancy grant awards within Vistula in the May 15 announcement detail — that's the most likely vector for accelerating tenant pipeline.

### New $23M Vistula Metropark advances
Metroparks Toledo is moving forward on what will be its 20th park — a $23M riverfront park along Water Street between Olive and Magnolia, with lagoons, fishing and kayak access, and Riverwalk system connection to Glass City Metropark. The Blade reported (April 22) that Metroparks has hired a design consultant. Some elements are expected to open later in 2026.

**Why it matters:** Public green space is the most consistent leading indicator for private investment in adjacent parcels in Toledo — 13abc's March coverage explicitly tied the new park to private investment activity in Vistula. Expect land-banking and pre-development activity within a 4–6 block radius of Water Street to pick up over the next 12 months.

### Riverfront soccer stadium proposal still in play
A Toledo group is pitching an $83.6M, 7,500-seat soccer stadium on the Vistula riverfront, across from Glass City Metropark.

**Why it matters:** This is speculative until financing and an anchor team are confirmed, but a stadium of this size is the kind of project that can either pull forward Vistula's transformation by 5+ years or get shelved entirely. Worth tracking quietly; not yet worth planning around.

## Uptown / Junction

### Connecting Toledo Neighborhoods to Opportunity — $53M, 38-block redevelopment
The federally-funded $53M streetscape project covering 38 blocks in Uptown and Junction (13th–21st Street, Adams to Monroe) is in its design phase through 2026, with construction running 2027–2029 and final completion targeted for 2031. The project will repave streets, add bike lanes and shelters, replace water and sewer lines, plant 2,300 trees, and install nearly 200 LED lights. A Cultural Trail along Dorr Street will connect Mott Branch Library to the 17th Street cycle track with public art gateways. Funded by a $20M USDOT RAISE grant.

**Why it matters:** This is the biggest neighborhood-scale infrastructure investment in this part of Toledo in a generation. The Junction neighborhood currently has a 45% vacancy rate (1,700 vacant lots, 400+ vacant structures out of ~4,700 parcels), which means the infrastructure is being put in *ahead of* private development rather than in response to it — a deliberate "build it and they will come" bet. The 2026 design milestones are where developers will start positioning. Anyone with a stake in Uptown/Junction parcels should pay close attention to the design-phase community meetings this year.

## Downtown

### Owens Corning commits $250M and 30-year downtown HQ stay
In exchange for a property tax incentive extension the city is pursuing (Blade, April 7), Owens Corning will invest $250M in building and technology improvements, create 25 new jobs, maintain its 1,036 existing high-wage jobs, and commit to keeping its world headquarters in downtown Toledo for 30 more years.

**Why it matters:** The downtown anchor-tenant story is the single most consequential variable for downtown property values and lunch-trade businesses. A 30-year commitment removes a tail-risk that has hung over downtown for two decades. Adjacent service businesses (lunch spots, parking operators, downtown residential) just got a much firmer floor under their planning assumptions.

### ConnecToledo's $120K music series grant — three-year programming runway
ConnecToledo was awarded $120K in matching funds to run a free outdoor music series across Promenade Park, Glass City Metropark, Junction Park, and Danny Thomas Park through 2026, 2027, and 2028. Partners include the City, Metroparks Toledo, and Toledo Design Collective.

**Why it matters:** A three-year programming commitment with named venues spanning Junction through downtown is the kind of soft infrastructure that builds cross-neighborhood foot traffic patterns. The choice to anchor a series in Junction Park alongside the higher-traffic Promenade is notable — it's an active attempt to normalize traveling to Junction.

### Mind and Soul Gallery & Studios opens in the Gardner Building
The Mind and Soul Gallery & Studios held its grand opening Saturday, May 16, in the Gardner Building on Madison Avenue.

**Why it matters:** Single openings are small data points individually, but the Madison Avenue corridor activation pattern is the story to watch — each new ground-floor tenant on Madison either reinforces the gallery/creative cluster or doesn't. Track whether the next 2–3 Gardner Building tenants share that creative-economy character.

## Citywide business climate

### Small business application growth
Toledo officials are pointing to a 7%+ rise in business applications nationwide in January as a leading indicator for steady local small business growth in 2026.

**Why it matters:** National application growth is a soft signal at best for Toledo specifically — but the city is using it publicly to frame 2026 as a growth year, which shapes how the Vibrancy and Planning Grant pipelines get talked about politically. If a city official cites this number to you, treat it as a directional optimism marker, not a Toledo-specific data point.

### ProMedica / Medical Mutual network dispute
WTOL is reporting a possible disruption in which Medical Mutual may drop ProMedica as an in-network provider in the Toledo area.

**Why it matters:** Not core to economic development, but ProMedica is a major downtown employer and any insurance-network turbulence affects workforce attraction. Worth a watching brief.
