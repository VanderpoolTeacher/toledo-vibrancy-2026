## Top story: OPmobility announces 541-job automotive exterior plant in Rossford — French supplier brings bumpers and tailgates for GM, Stellantis, Ford to I-75 corridor

OPmobility — a French automotive supplier formerly known as Plastic Omnium — announced June 1, alongside Governor Mike DeWine and JobsOhio, that it will build its first Midwest Exterior solutions facility in Rossford, Wood County. The approximately 600,000-square-foot plant is located on the west side of Glenwood Road south of Buck Road, just off I-75. It will produce plastic automotive exterior components — including bumpers and tailgates — for General Motors, Stellantis, and Ford. The announcement is backed by Ohio Tax Credit Authority approval and JobsOhio assistance; the City of Rossford made no direct financial contribution. Construction was already under way at the site at the time of the naming announcement.

The facility is projected to create more than 541 full-time direct jobs with $40 million in annual payroll by 2030, operating across three shifts five to six days per week. Production is targeted for the second half of 2027. An April 2026 announcement had flagged that an unnamed automotive manufacturer was coming to Rossford with 'nearly 1,000' total jobs expected including construction and indirect positions; the June 1 press release from OPmobility identified the company and confirmed the 541+ direct-employment figure. RGP posted the press release on its news hub, confirming regional EDO involvement in the site-selection process.

**Why it matters:** OPmobility is Europe's largest dedicated automotive exterior supplier — a company with roughly $9 billion in global revenue that has chosen the Toledo MSA's I-75 corridor for its first Midwest exterior plant. The 541 direct jobs and $40M payroll are the headline, but the supplier-network signal is equally important: bumpers and tailgates for GM, Stellantis, and Ford means OPmobility has locked in long-term vehicle-program contracts running through the 2020s production cycle, anchoring northwest Ohio's Tier-1 supply chain through the EV-transition period. The Rossford location is immediately south of Toledo on I-75 — the same labor market, same supplier ecosystem. JobsOhio's involvement confirms this was a competitively recruited win. Watch for the formal groundbreaking and the Ohio Tax Credit Authority incentive disclosure, which will reveal the full public investment behind the win.

### Toledo council's June 2 special session reconvenes on west Toledo water tower permit — vote today settles Kokosing contract fate
_Source: 2026-05-26_

Toledo City Council voted 5–6 on May 26 against the special use permit needed for two 2-million-gallon water towers planned for Northover Road near Silver Creek Elementary School in west Toledo — the most contested portion of the city's $85 million, four-tower water infrastructure expansion. McPherson, Morris, Gadus, Kramer, and Jones voted against the permit; Martinez, Williams, Komives, Mac Driscoll, Sarantou, and Hobbs voted for it. The rejection pauses the Northover Road work while the remaining two tower sites — Schneider Park on the south side and Holland-Sylvania Road and West Sylvania Avenue — are unaffected.

Kokosing was awarded the contract for the full project, and city officials warned that continued delay risks losing the contractor entirely — which would require a rebid at potentially higher cost. Crews had already begun clearing trees at the Northover Road site before the vote. Council member Theresa Morris, whose district includes the site, has opposed the location throughout: 'I really do feel that my constituents were done dirty, and I think the city didn't do due diligence in looking at other locations.' Nearby residents cited concerns about property values, neighborhood aesthetics, and loss of green space.

Toledo City Council convenes its special session today, June 2, at 1:55 p.m. at One Government Center to reconsider Ordinance 225-26 — the special use permit for the Northover Road towers. Under Ohio's procedural rules for reconsideration, a simple majority of the 11-member council is required to approve the permit. A successful re-vote clears Kokosing to proceed and preserves the full $85M project on schedule; a second rejection leaves the city with no current path on the Northover Road sites and would trigger either a rebid process or a hunt for alternative tower locations. Vote results are expected to be published by WTOL and The Blade today.

**Why it matters:** The $85M water tower project is designed to increase pressure across Toledo's distribution system and provide emergency reserve capacity as demand grows — the same infrastructure that underpins the city's site-readiness pitch for industrial and commercial development. RGP has spent more than a year marketing northwest Ohio to hyperscale data-center operators; data centers require stable, high-capacity water supply for cooling. A partially blocked water infrastructure expansion — particularly one with contractor-loss risk — directly undercuts the credibility of the site-readiness narrative that TCIC and LCReady are selling. The 5–6 vote margin is close enough that a reconsideration, compromise siting, or council re-vote could salvage the towers — but every month of delay compresses the Kokosing window and raises the cost of resolution.

### Toledo Yacht Club faces June 10 sheriff's sale — 160-year-old Point Place landmark defaulted on loan as community rallies
_Source: 2026-05-16_

The historic Toledo Yacht Club — a 160-plus-year-old waterfront landmark in Toledo's Point Place neighborhood — is scheduled for a Lucas County Sheriff's sale on June 10, 2026 at 10:00 a.m. via RealAuction. A January 2026 court judgment found the club defaulted on a 2015 commercial promissory note with Croghan Colonial Bank (originally $350,000), leaving an outstanding balance of approximately $263,164 plus 8.25% default interest accruing since 2023. The Lucas County Sheriff's Office received the Order of Sale in April; an appraisal of the property has already been completed.

The club is navigating compounding structural deterioration — roof replacement, dock repairs, stucco and exterior restoration, window replacement, and HVAC upgrades are all needed — alongside the financial default. WTOL's community-focused coverage found north Toledo residents and Councilmember Theresa Morris rallying around the club: 'It's just devastating for our community,' Morris said. Under the sale terms, the auction can be halted if the debt is paid or a settlement is reached before June 10. 13abc and WTOL first reported the situation in May 2026.

**Why it matters:** A 160-year-old private waterfront club is a niche asset, but its disposition sets a precedent for how Toledo handles historic waterfront parcels that fall into financial distress outside the formal land-bank pipeline. The Point Place location — on the Maumee River near Lake Erie — sits at the northern end of the city's waterfront story; a distress sale puts the property in the hands of whoever clears the judgment, with no guarantee of community-serving reuse. The debt balance is modest ($263K), meaning a motivated civic buyer, a preservation nonprofit, or even a club member coalition could theoretically satisfy it before the auction. If the sale proceeds, watch whether the buyer is a local operator or a passive LLC — that distinction will determine whether this becomes another Hotel Lorraine pattern or a legitimate reset.

### QTS Data Centers announces $10B, 902-acre campus in Van Wert — NW Ohio lands its largest-ever data center investment

Groundbreaking is targeted for Q4 2026; the campus is not expected to be fully operational until 2032. The project is projected to generate more than $200 million in tax revenue over 20 years and to create approximately 1,500 construction jobs and 200 permanent full-time positions at QTS once operations begin.

QTS is partnering with American Electric Power (AEP) to secure long-term reliable power for the campus. The VW Independent reported the construction and operations timeline details following the initial announcement.

**Why it matters:** The Van Wert QTS campus is the single largest capital investment ever announced in northwest Ohio's 17-county RGP footprint — by a wide margin. Dean Monske and Gary Thompson have spent years making the public case that NW Ohio's land availability, energy infrastructure, and cooling-water access make it competitive for hyperscale data-center investment; the Meta $800M campus in Wood County was the proof-of-concept, and Van Wert is the validation at a 12× larger scale. The announcement lands in the same week as the AI Ready Ohio Toledo expansion and ahead of EVIS America in November — the combined signal is that northwest Ohio's AI-economy positioning is converting from pitch to reality. For Toledo specifically, the Van Wert win raises the regional profile that site selectors see when they evaluate the broader market, and the AEP power partnership is a template for how future Toledo-area data-center deals might be structured. Watch for any Toledo-adjacent site announcements that follow; RGP rarely announces a single win without having other conversations active.

### RKP Group advancing $190M Four Corners redevelopment — Spitzer and Nicholas buildings in active demolition phase downtown
_Source: 2025-10-21_

The Lucas County Land Bank's board voted in October 2025 to authorize pre-development agreements with RKP Group for the restoration of the historic Spitzer and Nicholas buildings at Toledo's Four Corners — the intersection of Madison Avenue and Huron Street, the only downtown corner where all four original buildings are still standing. RKP Group will have the exclusive right to redevelop both buildings if conditions are met. The $190 million project calls for approximately 400 new residential units — 180 to 200 in the Spitzer building and 200 to 220 in the Nicholas building — plus an active ground-floor commercial corridor across more than 500,000 square feet of historic office space. RKP Group has led several major Toledo projects over the past 15 years, including Standart Lofts, The Berdan, Fort Industry Square, and The Overmyer, collectively adding more than 350 residential units to downtown.

Interior demolition on the Spitzer and Nicholas buildings began in summer 2025 and is expected to continue through spring 2026. More than $5 million in public funds has been deployed for asbestos abatement, roof stabilization at the Spitzer building, and engineering work, with total public investment projected to exceed $15 million by end of 2026. The project benefits from nearly $20 million in state historic tax credits — $9.6 million awarded to Spitzer in June 2024 and $10 million to Nicholas in December 2023. Reconstruction on the Spitzer building is tentatively targeted to begin in 2026, with completion projected for 2028; the Nicholas building is slated for 2027–2028 construction, with a possible 2029–2030 opening. A previous pre-development team (ARK Developments and The Model Group) stepped back in 2025 due to factors beyond their control; the historic tax credits they secured transferred to the current effort.

**Why it matters:** The Spitzer and Nicholas buildings are among Toledo's most recognizable structures and have sat largely vacant for years — a conspicuous gap at the city's most historic downtown intersection. A 400-unit residential conversion at Madison and Huron would double the residential footprint of the existing downtown core and place hundreds of new residents within walking distance of the Warehouse District, Huron Yards, and the Glass City Riverwalk spine. RKP Group is the right developer for this: its track record in Toledo's adaptive-reuse projects removes the execution-risk question that stopped previous teams. The $15M+ in public investment this year — on top of $19M in state historic tax credits — signals that the Land Bank and state preservation programs are fully committed. Watch for the Spitzer financing stack to close in late 2026, which is the real gating event for whether the 2028 completion target is achievable.

### Swayne Field Shopping Center renovation nearing completion in Englewood — $3.6M federal investment targets workforce training anchor and minority-owned businesses
_Source: 2026-05-26_

The Swayne Field Shopping Center in Toledo's Englewood neighborhood is nearing completion of a $3.6 million federally funded rehabilitation, The Blade reported May 26. The renovation is funded through $1.5 million in federal community project funds secured by U.S. Rep. Marcy Kaptur, a $1 million carryover in Community Development Block Grant funds, a $28,000 grant from the Toledo Community Foundation, and a Department of Energy Enhancement and Innovation Grant. **Why it matters:** Commercial corridor rehabilitation in legacy neighborhoods like Englewood mirrors the same thesis driving Vibrancy grants elsewhere in Toledo — federal and municipal anchor investment unlocks private tenant recruitment and neighborhood confidence. The workforce training center anchor is the highest-leverage piece: it seeds daily foot traffic independent of retail sales cycles and binds the site to workforce-development partners across the city. If Swayne Field's tenant mix stabilizes around the training center plus minority-owned storefronts, it becomes a replicable model for Toledo's other aging shopping plazas along Detroit, Alexis, and Sylvania Avenues that haven't seen recapitalization in decades.
