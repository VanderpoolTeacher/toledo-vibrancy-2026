## Top story: Glass City Riverwalk hits 50% complete — Watershed Weekend June 11–14 will mark the halfway milestone

Metroparks Toledo has scheduled a four-day celebration June 11–14 along Glass City Riverwalk to mark the halfway point of the 10-year, $250 million project, which now connects six Toledo neighborhoods along the Maumee River. The weekend opens June 11 at **The Bend** — the new elevated trail behind One SeaGate that links the Riverwalk to the bike/pedestrian lane on the MLK Bridge — with remarks from community leaders. Saturday's **River Crossing Expo** will close the MLK Bridge to motor traffic so the deck can host rock climbing, paddling in a large pool, mountain biking and other outdoor-skills demonstrations. The weekend also includes an Art Loop anchored by *Starburst*, a 15-foot illuminated sculpture; the TECHLEDO electronic music festival at The Ribbon and Market Hall; an international vendor fair; and a 5K with BRB Run Club.

The 50% completion marker is more than a programming hook. The Riverwalk is the spine of every other downtown-adjacent investment thesis the city is selling — Glass City Metropark, the future Vistula Metropark, the proposed Vistula soccer stadium, Owens Corning's new 30-year commitment, the proposed urban campground on the East side, and the Crane Development buildout in the Warehouse District all sit within (or are explicitly being marketed against) this connective tissue. The first half of the Riverwalk is targeted for completion this summer; the full build is expected to wrap by 2032.

**Why it matters:** This is the single most consequential piece of soft infrastructure shaping Toledo's downtown development thesis. The halfway-point celebration is the city's chance to convert a 10-year construction story into a public-facing brand moment — and to lock in private-investor confidence for the second half of the build. Anyone making placement decisions in Vistula, the Warehouse District, or East Toledo should plan to be visibly present that weekend. If the event draws well, expect a renewed push on the next round of Riverwalk-adjacent land assemblies; if it underperforms, expect a quieter pivot back to construction milestones.

## Warehouse District

### Swanky Scoops opening a Warehouse District scoop shop
The Blade reported (May 16) that Alissa Vieira's artisanal ice cream business, Swanky Scoops, is opening a brick-and-mortar shop in the Warehouse District — graduating from its production-only kitchen at 130 10th Street into a destination retail space with more square footage, more seating, expanded hours, and event programming.

**Why it matters:** Swanky Scoops is a small ticket on its own, but it lands in the right place at the right time — the Warehouse District is being reframed as a daytime/family-traffic destination through Huron Yards' grocery and restaurant additions and the upcoming beer garden. A scoop shop is exactly the kind of ground-floor tenant that turns "people pass through here for ballgames" into "people walk here on weekends." Track which block they sign in — a Huron Yards-adjacent address would reinforce the Erie Street cluster; a Hensville-adjacent address would diversify the entertainment-zone tenant mix.

## Vistula District / Ostrich Towne

### Ostrich Towne tenant pipeline expanding beyond the original two
Beyond the existing operating tenants (Molly's Irish Pub and Avestruz Tapas & Tequila), Ostrich Towne's published roster shows **Riverside BBQ Company** targeting an April opening and **CuriosiTEA Emporium** expected later in 2026. Together with Watershed Weekend traffic on the adjacent MLK Bridge in June and the start of the Glass City Riverwalk extension to the Vistula side this summer, the district is on track for its first real test of foot-traffic demand.

**Why it matters:** The Ostrich Towne thesis has been "two anchors plus a Vibrancy/Riverwalk tailwind equals a destination district." The next two tenant openings are the second leg of that thesis. If Riverside BBQ and CuriosiTEA both open and survive their first summer, the district will have shifted from "novelty" to "neighborhood" — and the developer's leasing pitch to the next 3–5 tenants gets materially easier. Watch the Vibrancy 19 property list (still pending detailed publication) to see whether any of those grants land inside Vistula and feed this pipeline.

### Soccer stadium proposal — new financing details surface
New filings reviewed by The Blade (April 14) clarify what's actually being pitched: the $83.6M, 7,500-seat stadium is being advanced by **Summit Street Sports** on dormant land off Summit Street near Toledo Pickle Company, directly across the river from Glass City Metropark. The group is targeting a United Soccer League franchise and a 2028–29 opening, with capacity to host 140+ events per year (matches plus concerts). Both Summit Street Sports and the Toledo Mud Hens organization (for Fifth Third Field renovation) applied for a share of Ohio's new $1 billion sports facility fund.

**Why it matters:** The stadium has moved from "trial balloon" to "filed proposal competing for state dollars," which is a different category of project to track. The 140-event capacity figure is the real argument — at that volume the stadium functions as a year-round programming engine for Vistula, not just a 17-match-per-year venue. The state award timing is now the gating decision. If Summit Street Sports lands a meaningful slice of the $1B fund, this becomes a 2028 anchor for the entire Vistula thesis; if it doesn't, expect the proposal to slow materially or be re-cut.

### Vistula Metropark — carry-forward

## Uptown / Junction

### Innovation Post tenant slate firms up
The Innovation Post — the centerpiece commercial space inside the $53M Connecting Toledo Neighborhoods to Opportunity footprint — will initially house **four named tenants**: Wurtec Incorporated, WorkSpring, Junior Achievement of Northwestern Ohio, and SEGULA Technologies USA. Together they span industrial supply (Wurtec), flexible office (WorkSpring), workforce/education (Junior Achievement), and engineering services (SEGULA — a French-headquartered global engineering firm). City planners separately note that roughly 67% of Uptown is currently parking lots and green space, the basis for the city's "extraordinary opportunity for new development" framing during the RAISE design phase.

**Why it matters:** This is the first time the Uptown redevelopment story has named-tenant specificity rather than infrastructure-and-vision rhetoric. Four tenants is enough to start sketching the second-order effects — lunch and service business demand, daytime population shift, parking absorption. SEGULA Technologies in particular is the kind of name that signals to other engineering and advanced-manufacturing companies that Uptown is viable, which compounds. The 67% parking/green-space statistic is the political framing developers should expect to hear repeatedly through the 2026 design-phase community meetings.

### RAISE / Connecting Toledo Neighborhoods to Opportunity — carry-forward
$53M project, 38 blocks (13th–21st, Adams to Monroe), $20M USDOT RAISE grant. Pre-design and engagement ran 2024–2025; design phase covers 2025–2026; construction 2027–2029; completion target 2031. No new design-phase community meeting dates announced this window.

## Downtown

### Glass City Metropark urban campground — opening 2026
Metroparks Toledo is moving into the build phase on a new urban campground on the East Toledo riverfront, sited between the National Museum of the Great Lakes and the Craig Memorial Bridge, adjacent to the Glass City Marina that Metroparks already operates. The campground will be relatively small but full-service — RV hookups, tent sites, cabins, and a pavilion. Funding stack: a Land and Water Conservation Fund federal grant pays for the access road and campsites; a ~$1M grant from the Lucas County Land Bank (out of the $3.2M the Land Bank received from the State of Ohio) covers site cleanup. Per Chief Outreach Officer Matt Killam, the campground is part of the broader Glass City Riverwalk project and is positioned to connect to destinations on both sides of the river.

**Why it matters:** An urban riverfront campground is an unusual asset for a Midwestern downtown — and the fact that boaters from the adjacent marina can also use the pavilion makes this a meaningfully different visitor pattern than Toledo currently captures. It also locks in another contiguous public-investment parcel along the East Toledo riverfront, which is the next neighborhood in line after Vistula for the Riverwalk-driven private-investment effect. Expect East Toledo land assemblies and small-business positioning to start showing up in the next 12 months.

### Owens Corning tax extension — vote watch
April reporting indicated a final City Council vote was expected at the end of April; no formal vote outcome has surfaced in this window's coverage. New detail from the negotiation already in the public record: alongside the 30-year HQ commitment and the $250M investment, Owens Corning will enter a payment-in-lieu-of-taxes agreement with Toledo Public Schools and a separate agreement with the city and Metroparks Toledo allowing the **final phase of the Glass City Riverwalk to be built on the Owens Corning campus**.

**Why it matters:** The Owens Corning campus easement is the under-reported detail. It means the Riverwalk's terminal phase is no longer dependent on a future negotiation — it's bundled into the same deal that gives Owens Corning its tax extension. That removes one of the largest tail risks on completing the 10-year Riverwalk build by 2032. Track the council vote outcome; if it stalls, the Riverwalk completion math gets harder.

## Citywide

### Vibrancy Initiative — five-year context for the 19 May 15 awards
Newly visible in the program-history framing: since the Vibrancy Initiative launched in 2021, it has invested in 100+ projects and leveraged $135M+ in private-sector capital into Toledo redevelopment. The 2026 cycle ($630K pool → 19 projects → est. $13.5M private spend, announced May 15) is on-trend rather than an outlier — the program's leverage ratio has held in the 20–25× range across cycles.

**Why it matters:** The 22× leverage figure on the 2026 cycle reads better when paired with the multi-year context: the city isn't claiming a one-off; it's claiming a repeatable program at scale. If you're modeling future commercial-corridor investment in Toledo, the right base case is "$600–650K of city money per cycle drawing $13–15M of private spend" — and that's the framing the Department of Economic Development is using publicly to justify the budget.
