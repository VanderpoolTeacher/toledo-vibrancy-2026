## Top story: Old West End Festival shooting — Day 10: Second suspect arrested per WTOL; Crisp-Carr case moves toward Common Pleas

At approximately 5:30 p.m. on June 6, 2026, gunfire erupted near the 53rd annual Old West End Festival in Toledo's historic Old West End neighborhood — at the intersection of Delaware and Glenwood avenues. Toledo Police reported at least 12 people shot, two of them critically; victims ranged in age from 14 to 61, with most in their early 20s. Investigators believe at least two shooters opened fire, likely aiming at each other. Extensive security was in place — additional officers, off-duty security personnel, and mobile surveillance cameras linked to the department's real-time crime center — yet the shooting proceeded. All 12 victims are expected to survive. No suspects were in custody as of the evening of June 7.

Mayor Wade Kapszukiewicz described the incident as 'over the top' in terms of violence. Sunday's festival events were cancelled following coordination between organizers, Toledo law enforcement, and the city. Governor DeWine, Rep. Marcy Kaptur, and other elected officials issued public reactions. WTOL reported that Toledo Public Safety Director George Kral urged residents and attendees to share any photos or video taken at the festival. The story received national wire-service pickup and was covered by CNN, NBC, ABC, and Fox News — placing Toledo in a national public safety narrative at the start of summer festival season.

The Old West End Festival is one of Toledo's oldest and highest-attendance annual outdoor events, drawing thousands of visitors to the nationally recognized historic neighborhood adjacent to downtown. The shooting comes 5 days before Watershed Weekend (June 11–14), Metroparks Toledo's marquee 40-event celebration of the Glass City Riverwalk's 50% completion milestone. As of June 7, no adjustments to the Watershed Weekend schedule have been announced; the full event program remains on. Watch security posture and attendance turnout at Watershed Weekend as the first public signal of whether Toledo's outdoor-event confidence holds through the incident.

As of June 8 — Day 2 — Toledo police confirmed all 12 victims are in stable condition; the two previously listed as critically injured have stabilized. No suspects are in custody and no arrests have been made; investigators are following multiple leads and urging anyone with photos or video from the festival to contact Crime Stoppers at 419-255-1111. The Old West End Association hosted a community support and resources session at Collingwood Presbyterian Church on June 8 from noon to 1 p.m. for those affected. Watershed Weekend — now 3 days out — remains fully scheduled; as of this brief, Metroparks Toledo has not issued a public statement on enhanced security posture for the June 11–14 event.

As of June 9 — Day 3 — Toledo police confirmed the detention of an 18-year-old male suspect in connection with the shooting, the first confirmed detention in the investigation. Separately, Mayor Kapszukiewicz, Toledo City Council, and Toledo Police held a press conference Tuesday morning to detail a summer safety and youth engagement strategy in the wake of the OWE incident. In coordination with that announcement, Metroparks Toledo publicly outlined what it described as its most extensive security plan to date for Watershed Weekend — a multi-agency posture involving the Toledo Police Department, Lucas County Sheriff's Office, private security contractors, and Metroparks' own internal police force. Watershed Weekend opens Thursday June 11, now 2 days away, and the full schedule of nearly 40 events remains in place.

As of June 10 — Day 4 — the 18-year-old taken in for questioning on June 8 was released after questioning with no charges filed. Toledo Police Chief Mike Troendle provided new investigative detail on June 9: the shooting stemmed from a dispute between two rival groups that escalated into a foot chase and physical altercation before gunfire. Three of the 12 victims were involved in the altercation; nine were struck by crossfire. Nine of the 12 victims have been released from the hospital. Two suspects are described as Black males approximately 18 to 24 years old; two firearms were recovered at the scene. Troendle said police have identified several persons of interest and hope to make an arrest 'very soon.' Lucas County prosecutors are seeking 'swift and strong' justice. At Tuesday's City Council meeting, council recognized June 2026 as National Gun Violence Awareness Month through a resolution sponsored by council members Kramer and Morris, praised the Mayor's Office of Neighborhood Safety and Engagement violence interrupters as 'some of the best in the nation' (per Toledo-Lucas County Health Department childhood wellness director Kendra Kec), and called on the state for additional public safety resources. The Blade reported June 9 that community activists separately convened to discuss strategies for fighting gun violence in Toledo.

As of June 11 — Day 5 — Toledo Police issued an arrest warrant for Ka Nye Taylor, 20, wanted on 11 counts of Felonious Assault in connection with the June 6 shooting. Taylor is described as a Black male, 5'11", 130 lbs, with black hair and brown eyes. Separately, a 17-year-old Whitmer High School graduate — already on probation for a prior gun charge — appeared before Judge Amy Stoner facing charges of tampering with evidence; he is accused of removing the weapon allegedly used in the shooting and driving it away from the scene. Police continue to search for a second shooter and have released a photograph seeking public identification. Crime Stoppers is offering up to $10,000 and the U.S. Marshals Service up to $5,000 for information leading to arrests. Despite the open investigation, Watershed Weekend opened on schedule this morning with a Rise and Rally kickoff at The Bend behind One SeaGate.

As of June 12 — Day 6 — Eljay Crisp-Carr, 20, was arrested on June 11 at a family member's home on 11 counts of felonious assault — the first arrest in the six-day investigation. Court records reviewed by The Blade show Crisp-Carr was already barred for life from possessing a firearm at the time of the shooting. Charging documents allege he fired into a crowd during the altercation; police affidavits state that Ka Nye Taylor began firing into the group first, and that as Crisp-Carr separated from the fight he turned toward Taylor and opened fire. Ka Nye Taylor remains at large; the active warrant for Taylor on 11 counts of felonious assault stands. Crime Stoppers (up to $10,000) and U.S. Marshals Service (up to $5,000) rewards remain in effect. Watershed Weekend Day 2 is underway on June 12 — Party in the Park at Promenade Park with multiple bands, a hot-air balloon glow on both sides of the Maumee, and the first official lighting of the MLK Bridge are all scheduled for this evening; multi-agency security posture remains in place for all events.

As of June 13 — Day 7 — Eljay Crisp-Carr appeared in Toledo Municipal Court on Friday, June 12. A judge set bail at $1.1 million and continued the case to Monday, June 15 at 9 a.m. Ka Nye Taylor remains at large; his warrant on 11 counts of felonious assault is active and both reward offers — Crime Stoppers ($10,000) and U.S. Marshals Service ($5,000) — remain in effect. Separately, WTOL reported that Old West End neighborhood residents are divided on whether the festival should be rescheduled for another date in 2026; no announcement of a rescheduled event has been made as of this brief.

As of June 14 — Day 8 — Eljay Crisp-Carr's next court hearing is scheduled for Monday, June 15 at 9 a.m. at Toledo Municipal Court. Ka Nye Taylor remains at large; his warrant on 11 counts of felonious assault is active and both reward offers — Crime Stoppers ($10,000) and U.S. Marshals Service ($5,000) — remain in effect. Separately, overnight June 12–13, three men were shot at the intersection of North Erie Street and Monroe Street in downtown Toledo during Watershed Weekend's penultimate night; all three suffered non-life-threatening injuries and police had no suspects in custody as of this brief. That downtown incident is unconnected to the OWE Festival investigation but reinforces the broader summer public safety backdrop under which Toledo is closing its highest-profile outdoor weekend.

As of June 15 — Day 9 — Eljay Crisp-Carr appeared for his scheduled 9 a.m. hearing at Toledo Municipal Court; specific case outcomes from that hearing — whether the matter was further continued, a preliminary hearing set, or bond conditions modified — were not publicly confirmed by the time of this brief. Ka Nye Taylor remains at large on 11 counts of felonious assault; the active U.S. Marshals warrant and both reward offers (Crime Stoppers up to $10,000, U.S. Marshals up to $5,000) remain in effect. The OWE investigation enters its second full week with one suspect in custody at $1.1 million bond and one shooter still sought. Watch for the Crisp-Carr case next to be transferred from Toledo Municipal Court to Lucas County Common Pleas for felony prosecution — that transfer signals the case moving into the full trial phase.

As of June 16 — Day 10 — WTOL reported that a second suspect in the Old West End Festival shooting has been arrested, with Toledo Mayor Wade Kapszukiewicz involved in the announcement. Given the investigation timeline, Ka Nye Taylor — wanted since the June 11 warrant was issued on 11 counts of felonious assault, with a U.S. Marshals fugitive warrant and a combined $15,000 reward active — is the most probable second suspect; the full article text was not accessible at time of publication to independently confirm his identity. If Taylor's apprehension is confirmed, both individuals believed to have exchanged gunfire that struck 12 people at the June 6 festival are now in custody. Eljay Crisp-Carr's June 15 hearing at Toledo Municipal Court has now occurred; the next expected procedural step is transfer of the Crisp-Carr case to Lucas County Common Pleas for felony prosecution, where a grand jury indictment on the 11 felonious assault counts would follow under Ohio law.

**Why it matters:** Toledo's outdoor festival economy is a pillar of the investment thesis being marketed to developers and site selectors — the argument that vibrant public spaces along the Maumee River drive private-sector confidence, foot traffic, and neighborhood activation. Watershed Weekend is the single most important outdoor-event proof point of that thesis in 2026. The OWE shooting — national coverage, 12 victims, suspects at large, Sunday cancelled — introduces a counter-narrative at a critical moment. Toledo's festivals have historically been safe; this is an outlier in the data, not a pattern. How the city performs in the days ahead — arrests, security posture for Watershed Weekend, mayoral messaging — will determine whether this incident becomes a footnote or a chilling factor in the outdoor-event confidence story that organizers and real estate developers alike are watching.

### Gordie Howe International Bridge opens June 15 — Canada proceeds over U.S. objections, Toledo's I-75 freight corridor unblocked
_Source: 2026-06-11_

The opening of the Gordie Howe International Bridge — the new $4.6 billion (Canadian) crossing linking Windsor, Ontario to Detroit, Michigan via Interstate 75 — was postponed on June 11 after the Trump administration blocked the planned June 13 ribbon-cutting ceremony. Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney confirmed the delay was 'at the request of the United States.' The Windsor-Detroit Bridge Authority abruptly canceled all opening festivities; no rescheduled date has been announced. The bridge was designed and funded entirely by Canada.

The Trump administration's stated concern centers on protecting the financial interests of the Moroun family — billionaire owners of the existing private Ambassador Bridge — from competition by the new publicly owned crossing. Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick and U.S. Ambassador to Canada Pete Hoekstra are reportedly seeking a negotiated arrangement to cushion the Moroun family's losses before the new bridge opens. Toledo leaders had explicitly anticipated economic benefits from the Gordie Howe crossing: the bridge connects to I-75 — the same highway spine that runs through Toledo — and was expected to increase commercial cross-border freight volumes in the corridor. Toledo EDOs have cited the bridge in site-selector materials for two years, pointing to the 40% share of Ohio exports that go to Canada and approximately 750,000 Canadians who pass through the Toledo area annually.

The Gordie Howe International Bridge opened to traffic on June 15, 2026 — nine days after the Trump administration blocked the June 13 ribbon-cutting. Canada announced June 8 that a traffic-only opening, without a bilateral ceremony, was set for June 15. The White House maintained that Trump's opposition 'has not changed,' but Canada proceeded: since Canada owns, built, and operates the bridge entirely, U.S. political pressure could delay a ceremony but could not indefinitely prevent the crossing from carrying traffic. Canadian Prime Minister Carney confirmed the opening as the week began; multiple news organizations — including Detroit News, Deadline Detroit, and Arab American News — confirmed traffic began on June 15. No formal bilateral ribbon-cutting has been rescheduled as of this brief.

For Toledo: the trigger event Toledo EDOs had tracked for two years — commercial freight flowing on the Gordie Howe Bridge — is now live. Sandy Spang, executive director of Lake Erie West Regional Council (formerly TMACOG), had said Toledo should prepare to capitalize on economic opportunities within the first year of the bridge's opening. The I-75 commercial freight corridor now connects to a second crossing at the Windsor-Detroit chokepoint with lower congestion than the Ambassador Bridge. Toledo-area manufacturers, logistics operators, and automotive suppliers serving Canadian OEMs can begin routing freight through the new span. The unresolved U.S.-Canada political tension is the open risk factor: any escalatory U.S. trade action targeting the crossing would reintroduce the corridor uncertainty Toledo EDOs have been selling against.

The bridge entered its first full weekday of commercial operation on June 16. The Canadian Trucking Alliance formally welcomed the opening, describing the new crossing as 'a transformative project for cross-border freight movement and North American supply chains.' The bridge is designed to handle up to 400 commercial vehicle crossings per hour across six lanes, with 167 acres of customs processing space on each side; the Windsor-Detroit corridor as a whole moves approximately one-quarter of all bilateral Canada-U.S. trade — more than $390 million CAD per day. Signature Associates, a regional commercial real estate firm, published analysis noting Toledo's readiness to absorb the freight and trade opportunities the bridge delivers. Early commercial freight volumes at the new span — versus sustained Ambassador Bridge traffic — are the leading operational indicator of whether the corridor shift Toledo EDOs have been selling to site selectors is materializing at the speed the pitch requires.

**Why it matters:** The bridge's June 15 opening — over White House objections, without a formal bilateral ceremony — delivers the infrastructure outcome Toledo's I-75 freight thesis has been waiting for. Toledo's investment pitch to manufacturers and logistics operators rests partly on I-75's role as the Detroit-to-Gulf industrial spine; the Gordie Howe crossing adds a second lane of commercial capacity at the Windsor-Detroit chokepoint and removes the structural freight constraint that benefits northwest Ohio warehousing, automotive supply chains, and cross-border distribution. What Toledo EDOs watch now: whether commercial freight volumes at the new crossing grow quickly enough to validate the marketing they've been doing; whether any U.S. trade escalation disrupts the opening momentum; and whether the bridge's operation at commercial scale produces the I-75 corridor activity spike that anchors the next round of site-selector conversations for Toledo-area industrial and logistics sites.

### Westgate Village North breaks ground — Trader Joe's leads seven-tenant roster at $60M west Toledo development
_Source: 2026-05-14_

Nordstrom announced on May 14 that it will open a 21,000-square-foot Nordstrom Rack at Westgate Village North — the first Nordstrom-brand store ever to serve the Toledo market. The store is scheduled to open in summer 2027. Westgate Village North is a 27-acre retail development at the northwest corner of Central Avenue and Secor Road (3311 Secor Road), developed by Abbell Associates in partnership with the Nikki Group on the former Elder-Beerman site. The Toledo-Lucas County Plan Commission approved the $75 million development in February 2026; the project calls for three to four retail and restaurant buildings aimed at 'new-to-market' national brands.

Nordstrom Rack President Gemma Lionello said the company looks forward to 'being a part of the Toledo community,' while Abbell CEO Liz Holland called Nordstrom 'best-in-class' and the anchor needed to 'bring a first-rate selection' to the west Toledo corridor. No other tenants had been named publicly at the time of the initial announcement.

Mayor Kapszukiewicz and Abbell CEO Liz Holland led a groundbreaking ceremony on June 10 at the Westgate Village North site. The city simultaneously announced the full tenant roster: Trader Joe's (Toledo's first location), Dick's House of Sport, Golf Galaxy, Sierra, Nordstrom Rack, Mission BBQ, and Smoothie King — all either new to Toledo or new to the northwest Ohio market. Also attending the ceremony: Toledo Chief Growth Officer Brandon Sehlhorst, District 5 City Council member Sam Melden, and Washington Local Schools Superintendent Kristine Martin. The total project cost is confirmed at $60 million.

Abbell Associates is partnering with The Nikki Group on an approximately $30 million redevelopment of the adjacent former Elder-Beerman site; additional retail tenants for that parcel are expected to be announced in coming months. No public opening timeline has been announced for either project.

**Why it matters:** Trader Joe's is Toledo's most-requested national retailer — it has appeared on community wish lists for years and repeatedly cited a market size gap as the barrier. Landing it as the anchor of a new-build development, alongside Dick's House of Sport, Golf Galaxy, and five other new-to-market names, signals that Toledo's demographic and income profile finally clears national retailers' underwriting thresholds. The $60M project on the former Sears site converts a long-vacant anchor pad into a full retail block in one move.
